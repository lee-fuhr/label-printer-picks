Label Printer questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Label printers simplify labeling for shipping, barcodes, and organization. This FAQ covers key differences between thermal and inkjet printers, connectivity options, label costs, and how to choose the right one for your needs.

What types of label printers are available?

The two main technologies are thermal and inkjet. Thermal printers use heat to darken specially coated label stock; they come in two flavors: direct thermal (no ribbon, but the labels can fade over time) and thermal transfer (uses a ribbon for more durable prints). Inkjet printers use liquid ink and can print in color on a wider range of label materials, but they require periodic cartridge replacements. For most shipping and barcode use, a direct thermal printer is simple and cost-effective; the labels are generally black-on-white. If you need color or archival quality (labels that must last for years), thermal transfer or inkjet is the better route. Keep in mind that direct thermal labels are prone to darkening if left in a hot car or direct sunlight for long periods.

How do I choose between wired and wireless connectivity?

Most label printers support USB for a direct wired connection to a computer – that’s reliable and works with nearly any operating system. For more flexibility, many models also offer Bluetooth or Wi-Fi. Bluetooth is great for printing from a phone or tablet without needing a network, while Wi-Fi lets you put the printer anywhere within range of your router and share it among multiple devices. Think about your workspace: if you frequently move your printer or need to print from different rooms, wireless is worth the extra cost. If the printer will sit next to a dedicated computer, USB is perfectly fine and usually cheaper. Some models can be connected via Ethernet for office networks as well.

What kind of labels can I use?

Label printers accept rolls or sheets of label stock in various sizes – from tiny address labels up to large 4x6 shipping labels. The material can be paper, polypropylene, polyester, or even vinyl, each with different durability and adhesion properties. For general use, paper direct thermal labels are the most affordable. For outdoor or industrial use, polyester or polypropylene labels are more water- and tear-resistant. Make sure the label width and length are compatible with your printer model. Many manufacturers sell proprietary label rolls, but there are also generic options that work well. If you need heavy-duty adhesive for rough surfaces, look for permanent adhesive labels; removable or repositionable labels are available for temporary applications.

How fast do label printers print?

Print speed is usually measured in inches per second (ips). Entry-level thermal printers often print between 4 and 6 ips, while industrial models can reach 12 ips or more. For occasional home or small office use, a speed of about 4 ips is perfectly adequate – you won’t notice a delay for single labels. High-volume shipping operations may benefit from faster speeds. Resolution also matters: most thermal printers offer 203 dpi (dots per inch), which is fine for basic barcodes and text. If you need very small text or high-definition graphics, a 300 dpi or even 600 dpi model gives sharper prints, though at a slightly slower speed. Balance your need for crispness against how many labels you print daily.

What is the cost of labels and maintenance?

The biggest ongoing expense is the label stock itself. Direct thermal labels are relatively inexpensive – a roll of about 500 standard 4x6 shipping labels can be budget-friendly. Thermal transfer labels cost more because you also need to replace the ribbon periodically. Inkjet printers have the highest consumable cost, as ink cartridges are pricey and the special label paper can be more expensive. Maintenance for thermal printers is minimal – just clean the print head occasionally with a special pen or alcohol wipe. Inkjet printers require more attention to prevent clogged nozzles. Overall, thermal printers (especially direct thermal) have lower total cost of ownership if you print frequently, while inkjets make sense for those who need occasional color labels.

Can I use a label printer for barcodes and shipping?

Absolutely – that’s one of the primary uses. Thermal barcode label printers are standard in logistics, warehouses, and retail because they produce sharp, scannable barcodes quickly. Most support common barcode symbologies like Code 128, Code 39, UPC, and EAN. For shipping, 4x6 labels are the default. Many printers integrate with popular shipping platforms such as Pirate Ship, ShipStation, or directly with carriers. If you need to print labels from your own inventory system, check that the printer supports ZPL (Zebra Programming Language) or EPL (Eltron Programming Language) – many thermal printers understand these commands. Consumer-friendly models from Brother or DYMO often come with their own software that can generate barcodes and shipping labels without any technical setup.

Frequently asked questions

How do I connect a label printer to my computer?

Most label printers connect via USB just like a standard printer. Plug in the cable, install the driver, and you are ready to print. Wireless models use Bluetooth or Wi-Fi – follow the manufacturer’s instructions to pair or connect to your network.

Can I print labels from my phone?

Yes, many newer label printers have Bluetooth or Wi-Fi and a companion app for iOS and Android. You can often print directly from a phone’s photo gallery, notes, or a supported shipping app. Printers without wireless connectivity cannot be used with a phone without a computer or a special adapter.

Do I need special software for a label printer?

Most label printers include basic printer drivers and a template editor. For advanced design – like custom barcodes, logos, or variable data – manufacturers provide software or you can use third-party programs like Bartender, NiceLabel, or online services. Generic thermal printers that support ZPL can be controlled directly by custom software.

What is DPI and do I need high resolution?

DPI stands for dots per inch, which determines sharpness. 203 dpi is standard and perfectly fine for most barcodes and text. If you print very small fonts, intricate graphics, or need high-density barcodes (like on tiny electronic components), choose 300 dpi or 600 dpi. The trade-off is slower speed and higher cost.

Are thermal printers better than inkjet?

For most labeling tasks, yes. Thermal printers have no ink to dry out or smudge, they are faster, quieter, and have lower consumable costs for high-volume printing. Inkjet printers win if you need color, glossy photo labels, or the ability to print on non-specialty paper. Choose based on whether you prioritize running costs and speed over color versatility.

How long do thermal labels last?

Direct thermal labels can last a few years in normal indoor conditions but may fade or darken if exposed to heat, sunlight, or certain chemicals. Thermal transfer labels, which use a ribbon, are much more durable and can last decades or more. For archival use, always choose thermal transfer or inkjet with pigment ink.

In shortChoosing the right label printer comes down to understanding your volume, durability needs, and connectivity preferences. Start by deciding between thermal and inkjet, then match the size and speed to your workload – you’ll find a reliable model that fits your budget and saves you time over hand-labeling.
